UCLA-backed venture

A peer-to-peer rental marketplace for college campuses — and the first time I owned a product end to end, from idea to build to investor pitch, out of the UCLA accelerator.

Role: co-founder·Year: 2017–2018·Backing: UCLA accelerator

What it was

A peer-to-peer rental marketplace built around college campuses — students renting the things they only need occasionally (gear, equipment, dorm and event stuff) to other students nearby, instead of buying new. The campus was the wedge: a dense, trust-rich, high-turnover community where the same items get bought and abandoned every semester. We leaned into that — verified student identities, pickup that worked at walking distance, and listings tuned to the rhythm of the school year.

Like any marketplace it was a two-sided problem: no renters without supply, no supply without renters. Seeding one campus at a time, getting liquidity in a single network before expanding, and designing the trust layer that makes strangers comfortable handing over their things — that was the real work. It's also where the "own the whole thing" instinct started: not a class project, a real product with real investors to answer to.

What I owned

  • Investor pitching — to angels and VCs, learning to tell a technical story to people writing checks.
  • Agile development — leading the build, deciding what shipped and what got cut.
  • Go-to-market — the first version of the muscle I'd later use to close enterprise deals.

What it taught me

Two things that still define how I work. First: the gap between a working demo and a real product is where most of the actual work lives — a lesson I've been paid to act on ever since. Second: an engineer who can also sit across from the buyer and the investor is worth more than either skill alone.

I learned to build the thing and tell its story in the same breath. That combination became the whole career.
